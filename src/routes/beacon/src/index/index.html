ABSTRACT

Avalanche beacons can often be the deciding factor between life and death in the event of an avalanche. Although proven to work, the overall efficiency of avalanche beacons has remained stagnant over the past several decades. Our goal as a team is to improve the efficiency of the avalanche beacon so that the process of finding a buried victim is much faster than its current state. Many strategies and approaches are currently being researched by our team in order to find out how exactly this goal can be accomplished. Some of our most promising approaches involve implementing a beacon with data that can be sent through radio waves, implementing an ad-hoc wireless network, and algorithms to calculate the final position of the victim based trajectory from the slide. Nevertheless, one of our team’s main priorities is to retain backwards compatibility in order to operate in unison with any beacon.

CURRENT AVALANCHE BEACONS

Modern avalanche beacons (transceivers) operate at a 457kHz frequency. All beacons have two modes in which they operate. Those two modes are transmit and receive. When a beacon is operating in transmit mode, the beacon emits a low-power pulse. When in receive mode, beacons will be able to pick up the pulse emitted from a different beacon operating in transmit mode. Whenever a beacon detects a signal, the beacon will point the user in the direction that the signal was transmitted from.

Some modern beacons have been implemented with technologies such as vitals detection. This allows users to determine whether a victim buried under the snow is alive or deceased. This gives the user the ability to continue searching for other buried victims instead of consuming valuable time unburying a deceased victim. Though this technology can be extremely useful in a lot of cases, it doesn’t improve the overall time in which it takes to find a buried victim.

CURRENT PROJECT STATUS

The team is currently working on the ad-hoc network on the ARM Cortex STM32072RBT M0 microcontroller. The parts have been ordered and interfacing with them has begun. We hope that by the end of the spring semester (2018), we will have a very basic implementation of the ad-hoc network working. This will be demonstrated through sending data between the boards. The data that will be sent between the boards will be gyroscope data that is collected on STM32F072RBT boards that we currently own, and sent to another STM32F072RBT board. Once the board receives this data it will be displayed to the computer screen that it is hooked up to via a USART connection so that we know that the data is being transmitted and received correctly.
